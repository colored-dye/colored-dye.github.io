Weak-to-Strong Alignment Transfer

transfer task alignment from previously trained but weaker models to new but stronger foundational model to kickstart post-training.

Created on April 13, 2026 , last updated on April 13, 2026

Contents

 ## Introduction Large language models (LLMs) possess strong reasoning capabilities and broad general knowledge, yet they remain imperfect across domains, often lacking the specialized capabilities required for specific tasks. Post-training is therefore essential to align them with target tasks and desired behaviors~\citep{bai2022training, ouyang2022training, zhou2023lima}. Prevalent paradigms for post-training include \textit{supervised fine-tuning (SFT)} and \textit{reinforcement learning (RL)}. However, SFT requires large quantities of high-quality labeled data and often memorizes patterns rather than learning generalizable reasoning strategies~\citep{chu2025sft}. It also suffers from catastrophic forgetting in multi-task settings, where adapting to new tasks degrades performance on earlier ones~\citep{shenfeld2025rl}. RL offers a more flexible alternative but suffers from significant inefficiency, primarily due to its \textbf{inability to effectively leverage external knowledge}. Specifically, traditional RL methods, such as \textit{group relative policy optimization (GRPO)}~\citep{shao2024deepseekmath}, rely on sparse outcome-level rewards, providing feedback only after full solution generation and thus leading to inefficient credit assignment~\citep{sutton1998reinforcement,wen2026reinforcement}. Recent RL approaches like \textit{process reinforcement through implicit rewards (PRIME)}~\citep{cui2025process} mitigate this reward sparsity issue by deriving dense token-level rewards via log-probability differences between a reward model and its reference. However, such methods still cannot incorporate external domain experts, as they operate in a \textit{symmetric setting} where the reward model and policy share the same base model. This constraint necessitates maintaining and jointly updating a large reward model, resulting in substantial computational overhead. As a result, RL-based alignment of large models remains prohibitively expensive, often requiring tens of thousands of GPU hours~\citep{yang2025qwen3}. Furthermore, multi-task learning compounds these challenges. Sequential RL on multiple tasks can lead to catastrophic forgetting~\citep{korbak2022reinforcement,zeng2026glm} while joint training with mixed rewards could suffer from gradient interference~\citep{wu2025imbalanced,wang2026mix}. We believe these challenges call for a \textbf{bootstrapping paradigm for post-training}, where previously aligned but weaker models are reused as supervision to efficiently transfer domain-specific capabilities into strong foundation models. This allows new models to inherit alignment from prior models as ``kickstart'' without costly RL from scratch, significantly reducing the time and compute needed for post-training. In this work, we aim to address a fundamental question: \textbf{\textit{How can we transfer domain-specific capabilities from weaker, previously aligned models to stronger general models in a scalable and effective manner?}} An emerging alternative, \textit{on-policy distillation (OPD)}, enables students to learn from their own mistakes under teacher supervision, provides dense token-level feedback while incorporating external knowledge priors and prevents forgetting in multi-task setups~\citep{agarwal2024onpolicy,gu2024minillm,lu2025onpolicydistillation}. By combining the exploration benefits of on-policy learning with the dense supervision of distillation, OPD overcomes reliance on large-scale annotated data and improves training efficiency, and has been shown to support multi-teacher capability integration without catastrophic forgetting~\citep{lu2025onpolicydistillation,yang2026learning}. However, existing OPD techniques are primarily designed for \textit{strong-to-weak} or \textit{peer distillation}, where the teacher is at least as capable as the student. This assumption breaks down in more practical settings where no stronger teacher is available. Instead, one must transfer knowledge from weaker domain-specific models to a strong general model—a paradigm known as \textit{weak-to-strong generalization (W2SG)}~\citep{burns2024weak}.
